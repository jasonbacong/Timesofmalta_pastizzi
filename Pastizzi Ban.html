National Pastizzi Ban - due to increased obesity rates in Malta

Government said 'We are pushing all that we can to improve our countries health by any means necessary...'

3 hours ago| Jason Grech |1033 min read
Robert Abela speaking during an interview aired on Sunday. Photo: PL.

In an unprecedented move to address Maltas growing obesity epidemic, Prime Minister Robert Abela announced this Sunday that the sale of pastizzia long-cherished national delicacywill be banned with immediate effect across the Maltese Islands.

The decision, which marks one of the most significant public health interventions in recent years, was made following new data presented by the Ministry for Health indicating that Malta now ranks among the highest in Europe for obesity and diet-related illness, including Type 2 diabetes and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

Speaking at a press conference outside Castille, Prime Minister Abela stated, This is not a decision taken lightly. For too long, we have ignored the long-term impact of our eating habits. Today, we draw a line. We are choosing the health of our people over tradition.

The immediate nationwide ban applies to the production, sale, and distribution of pastizzi, typically made with high-saturated-fat pastry and calorie-dense ricotta or pea fillings. The measure affects kiosks, cafs, supermarkets, and all public food establishments, with enforcement to begin as early as Monday morning.

Health Minister Dr. Joseph Deguara called the move a necessary and courageous step toward safeguarding future generations, pointing to decades of public health warnings that had, until now, failed to yield significant behavioural change.

The ban has already drawn mixed reactions across the country. While some hail it as long overdue, others argue it encroaches on national culture. Economists have also raised concerns over the economic impact on small businesses reliant on pastizzi sales for survival.

Despite the backlash, the government maintains the measure is only one part of a broader strategy. Officials confirmed that further dietary reforms and educational campaigns are planned in the coming months, including subsidies for healthier food options and an overhaul of the school canteen system.

Prime Minister Abela concluded his statement with a resolute message: We can no longer romanticise foods that are actively contributing to our nations health crisis. This is a moment of responsibilityand reform.
